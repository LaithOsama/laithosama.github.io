Ride the Tiger: A Survival Manual for the Aristocrats of the Soul

Julius Evola

Philosophy, Politics, Spirituality.

Chapter 1: The Crisis of Modernity

In the opening chapter of “Ride the Tiger” by Julius Evola, the author addresses the crisis of modernity and its impact on individuals and society. Evola critiques the decline of traditional values, the loss of spiritual meaning, and the rise of a materialistic and decadent culture.

From a traditional perspective, the crisis of modernity represents a departure from the timeless principles and virtues that once guided human civilization. Traditional thought emphasizes the need for individuals to resist the conformist pressures of modern society and to reconnect with transcendent values and higher truths.

Chapter 2: The Path of Inner Liberation

This chapter explores the path of inner liberation as a response to the crisis of modernity. Evola discusses the need for individuals to cultivate an inner detachment from the prevailing norms and values of society. He advocates for a transformative journey that involves self-mastery, discipline, and the pursuit of higher states of consciousness.

From a traditional perspective, the path of inner liberation is rooted in the quest for transcendence and self-realization. Traditional thought recognizes the importance of spiritual practices and the cultivation of virtue to transcend the limitations imposed by the material world.

Chapter 3: Active Nihilism

In this chapter, Evola explores the concept of active nihilism as a response to the crisis of modernity. He discusses the rejection of conventional morality and the embrace of a higher, individualistic code of conduct. Evola argues for the development of a select elite who can rise above the mediocrity of modern society.

From a traditional perspective, active nihilism represents a radical departure from traditional moral frameworks. Traditional thought emphasizes the importance of moral values and social harmony as foundations for a well-ordered society.

Chapter 4: The Dialectic of the Absolute

This chapter delves into the dialectic of the absolute, examining the interplay between the eternal and the temporal. Evola discusses the need to transcend dualistic thinking and embrace a higher reality beyond conventional notions of good and evil.

From a traditional point of view, the dialectic of the absolute recognizes the existence of a higher metaphysical reality that transcends human conceptions of morality and existence. Traditional thought emphasizes the pursuit of transcendent truths and the integration of the temporal and eternal aspects of human nature.

Chapter 5: Beyond Life and Death

In the final chapter, Evola explores the concept of transcending the limitations of life and death. He discusses the pursuit of immortality through a higher form of existence, detached from the cycles of birth and death.

From a traditional perspective, the quest for transcendence beyond life and death is deeply rooted in spiritual and metaphysical traditions. Traditional thought emphasizes the attainment of spiritual liberation and the transcendence of the limitations of the material world.

Book Recommendations

  1. “The King's Two Bodies” by Ernst H. Kantorowicz: This influential work examines the medieval concept of the king's two bodies, exploring the intertwining of political and religious authority in monarchic systems.
  2. “The City of God” by Saint Augustine: Written by one of the most important figures in Christian theology, this book explores the relationship between the earthly city and the City of God, shedding light on the theological underpinnings of political order.
  3. “On the Spirit of Laws” by Montesquieu: Montesquieu's classic work explores the separation of powers and the role of political institutions in maintaining a balanced and stable society, providing valuable insights into the relationship between law and politics.
  4. “The Leviathan” by Thomas Hobbes: Hobbes' seminal work presents a compelling argument for absolute sovereignty and explores the necessity of political authority to prevent a state of nature marked by chaos and conflict.
  5. “The Crisis of the Modern World” by René Guénon: Guénon's book offers a critique of modernity from a traditional perspective, analyzing the spiritual and metaphysical dimensions of the crisis facing contemporary civilization.
