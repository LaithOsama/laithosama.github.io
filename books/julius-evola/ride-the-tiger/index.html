Ride the Tiger

"Ride the Tiger" by Julius Evola is a complex and thought-provoking book that explores the concept of traditionalism and the challenges facing individuals in the modern world. While some of Evola's ideas may be controversial, the book offers valuable insights into the human condition and the need for individuals to cultivate a sense of inner strength and resilience in the face of cultural decline. Ultimately, the book encourages readers to embrace a more conscious and deliberate approach to life, one that is rooted in tradition, wisdom, and self-mastery.

Summary

"Ride the Tiger" is a book written by Julius Evola, an Italian philosopher and esotericist. Published in 1961, the book explores the challenges of the modern age and offers a critique of contemporary society from a traditionalist perspective. Here's a summary of the key ideas in "Ride the Tiger":

  1. Crisis of the Modern Age: Evola argues that modern society is in a state of crisis, characterized by spiritual emptiness, moral decline, and a loss of traditional values. He sees the modern world as lacking a higher purpose or transcendental principles.
  2. Traditionalism and Spiritual Revolt: Evola advocates for a revival of traditional values, spirituality, and metaphysical principles. He believes that a spiritual revolt against the prevailing materialistic and egalitarian values of modernity is necessary to restore a higher sense of purpose and meaning.
  3. The Dilemma of the Individual: The book explores the existential predicament of the individual in the modern world. Evola discusses the struggle of those who feel alienated by the prevailing norms and ideologies, yet are unable to fully embrace a traditional worldview. He refers to this state as "riding the tiger" – being caught in a world they cannot fully reject or embrace.
  4. Transcending Dualities: Evola suggests that one must transcend the dualities of modern existence, such as good and evil, individual and society, and action and contemplation. He proposes a path of self-transcendence that involves developing inner mastery and aligning oneself with higher spiritual realities.
  5. Critique of Politics and Culture: The book criticizes political ideologies, mass culture, and the erosion of traditional institutions. Evola views political involvement as ineffective in bringing about genuine change, advocating instead for individual transformation and the cultivation of an elite capable of preserving traditional values.
  6. Eastern Wisdom and Esoteric Traditions: Evola draws upon various Eastern philosophies, including Buddhism, Hinduism, and Taoism, as well as esoteric traditions, to provide insights into spiritual transformation and the overcoming of modern challenges.

"Ride the Tiger" presents a complex and often controversial perspective on the nature of modernity, spirituality, and the individual's role in society. It is considered one of Evola's most influential works, providing a critical examination of the modern world while advocating for a return to traditional values and spiritual transcendence.
