The Crisis of the Modern World

René Guénon

The Crisis of the Modern World is a philosophical book written by French author and metaphysician René Guénon in 1927. It serves as a critique of the modern Western civilization by delving into the problems that plague it, analyzing their roots, and offering an alternative traditional perspective built on the basis of esotericism, spirituality, and ancient wisdom. Guénon's work is seen as part of the Traditionalist School and seeks to bring attention to the divergence of modern society from traditional principles.

Summary:

In the book, Guénon identifies several key issues that he believes have led to the present crisis of the modern world. These include materialism, individualism, and secularism, which have eroded the sense of the sacred and led to a loss of higher knowledge and wisdom. By rejecting traditional spiritual values, modern civilization has fostered a superficial and misguided understanding of reality, which in turn has fueled feelings of alienation and discontent amongst people.

  1. Materialism: Guénon argues that the modern world is dominated by the belief in the supremacy of matter, which downplays or outright denies the existence of non-material realms. This exclusive focus on the material world has led to a predatory approach to nature and a purely quantitative outlook on life. People prioritize measurable "progress" and economic growth over spiritual and moral progress and lose sight of what truly matters.
  2. Individualism: Another consequence of the modern worldview is rampant individualism, which Guénon sees as a deeply flawed notion that divorces people from their communities, spiritual traditions, and the collective wisdom of the past. This emphasis on personal autonomy destabilizes society and fosters egocentrism, selfishness, and a lack of empathy for others.
  3. Secularism: The separation of religion and spirituality from other areas of life is yet another problematic aspect of modernity, according to Guénon. He posits that the secularization of society is a direct result of the rejection of traditional wisdom and spiritual insights. As a response, people turn to materialism and mass-consumer culture, which ultimately fails to provide meaning and purpose.

Guénon sees this crisis stemming from the ignorance of what he considers "true" tradition, which is described as the primordial and transcendent source of knowledge, wisdom, and spiritual truth. For Guénon, tradition incorporates essential metaphysical principles that must be understood in order for humanity to enjoy genuine stability, harmony, and fulfillment. He points to Eastern traditions and esoteric practices as potential resources for addressing the crisis and reorienting modern civilization towards its spiritual roots.

In conclusion, The Crisis of the Modern World is a critical examination of the prevailing trends and beliefs of Western civilization. René Guénon asserts that the abandonment of tradition, the decline of spirituality, and the focus on materialism have led to a widespread sense of crisis in the modern world. By embracing the perennial wisdom found in traditional teachings, Guénon argues that humanity can restore balance and meaning to their lives, and ultimately overcome the challenges and pitfalls of modernity.
