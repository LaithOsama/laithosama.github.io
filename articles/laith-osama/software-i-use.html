Software I use

This is my three-year journey into the world of "Free and Open Source Software" (FOSS). I explored various programs and discovered their hidden treasures. These programs, embody the simplicity and functionality. Their brilliance remains untarnished, unblemished by the superficial allure of utilitarian capitalism. Instead, they were born out of pure intention and sincere dedication, transforming mundane pieces of plastic into invaluable tools that serve their users with unparalleled efficiency.

In their existence, these programs bear the noble responsibility of redirecting the trajectory of software development, guiding it back onto the path of progress, with a wary eye toward the perils of undue complexity. A stark contrast emerges when compared to their counterparts, whose arduous evolution breeds a regrettable cycle of mounting intricacies and insatiable demands for computational resources.

Amidst my admiration for these remarkable creations, I must implore you to embrace a vital caveat-an admonition to approach this technology with temperance and discernment. Let us not succumb to an excessive reliance, but instead wield it judiciously and purposefully. With enlightened guidance, we possess the power to transform this technology from a potential adversary into a steadfast ally. By harnessing its vast capabilities, we can propel our endeavors forward, enrich our lives, and foster a newfound independence from the shackles of the modern world order and industrial society.

The distribution

Regarding Linux distributions, I have tried several over the course of two years. Mint, Lubuntu, Kubuntu, Opensuse, Fedora, Arch and Artix. I favored Arch/Artix, but then I discovered Void Linux. Ah, what a wondrous distro it was, surpassing all that came before!. After a year of using, I felt ashamed for using a distro led by some random nigger-tranny-faggot. Also, Void Linux proved to be excessive for my simple needs, with its intricate complexities impeding my ability to exercise complete control over its core parts.
And so, my quest for stability and harmony propelled me towards Oasis and KISS Linux, beckoning me towards new horizons of possibility. Kiss, with its simplicity and meticulously crafted design, bestowed upon its users unparalleled dominion over every constituent aspect. However, amidst this pursuit, I encountered Oasis, a true marvel of extraordinary beauty and unbounded astonishment. Its essence transcended the confines of mere perfection, encapsulating the essence of genius within its meticulously orchestrated design.

Throughout this journey, I realized that exploring many different distributions is a such waste of time. Many of them have marred by foolish and convoluted design choices, have strayed far from their original purpose, succumbing to the allure of emulating the Windows Look/Feel. As for the remaining few, they bear a striking resemblance to one another, their distinctions fading into obscurity.

However, amidst this sea of mediocrity, a select few distributions have emerged as beacons of hope, worthy of our attention and exploration. Such as "Void Linux", "Alpine Linux", "KISS Linux" and "Oasis Linux".

Display server

When it comes to display servers, I have embraced both Wayland and Framebuffer. Within Wayland, I use wld, swc, velox, st, and dmenu, each fulfilling a unique purpose. In the embrace the Framebuffer, I am accompanied by fbpad, fbff, fbpdf, and more, tools that illuminate my path.

Desktop environment

Ah, the tumultuous realm of Desktop Environments! A tempestuous landscape of instability and bloated excess. Even Windows itself is lighter and more stable compared to many Desktop Environments I encountered during my journey. Thus, I wandered between Windows and Linux, until the fateful encounter with xfce4, a sanctuary of stability amidst chaos. It stands as a shining example of a Desktop Environment, perchance the pinnacle of its kind. Yet, even in the midst of this discovery, a longing persisted within me, until I found dwm. Perfect, unyielding, and free from the shackles of bugs that plagued others (FUCK YOU KDE), dwm emerged as the epitome of speed and stability. It graced my ancient 12-year-old laptop with unwavering grace, a testament to its impeccable codebase. With kernel, services, and a terminal window, I marveled at a 30MB RAM consumption. Later, I realized, that the surplus of features and conveniences within the meticulously curated system I once embraced. It became apparent that many of these features were no longer essential, and I found myself capable of relinquishing their gasp. A weariness settled within me, weary of expending precious time on trivialities solely to satisfy aesthetic inclinations (ricing). In Light of this revelation, I harbored no hesitation in transitioning from Xorg for Wayland and embracing velox, recognizing its capacity to streamline my endeavors and restore focus to what truly matters.

The utils

  • Terminal: st, having bad times here as a BiDi user. However I use it in wayland using this port.
  • Web browser: chromium (ungoogled). Based on my vast experience, I dare assert that chromium, in its fastness and lightness, outshines the very fabric of Firefox.. It also shall be written better then firefox.
  • Video player: mpv, It's a powerful video player, it can be configured to consume the least of CPUs, also I use it as audio player and image viewer. I use Fbff to play short videos in Framebuffer, it use way less ram then mpv.
  • Editor: Neatvi/Vis, both are vi clones, They exude minimalism and usability, surpassing their forebears in elegance and functionality.
  • RSS Reader: sfeed, the based way to read RSS.
  • Docs Reader: embracing Mupdf in Xorg/Wayland, Fbpdf in Framebuffer.
